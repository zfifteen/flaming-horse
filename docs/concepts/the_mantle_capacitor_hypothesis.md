# Everyone Is Asking the Wrong Question About Mantle Earthquakes

## The mainstream explanation for deep continental earthquakes has a problem. What if the mystery was never about the rock at all?

***

In February 2026, Stanford researchers Shiqi (Axel) Wang and Simon Klemperer published something extraordinary in *Science*: the first global catalog of continental mantle earthquakes. They sifted through more than 46,000 seismic events dating back to 1990 and confirmed 459 earthquakes that originated not in the crust, where earthquakes are supposed to happen, but in the mantle below it.

The discovery made headlines everywhere. "Impossible earthquakes." "Mysterious quakes deep inside Earth." "First-ever global map of rare mantle earthquakes."

But every single headline, every press release, every explainer, and almost every expert quoted in the coverage framed the discovery around the same question:

*How can rock that should flow actually break?*

That question has dominated the field for over two decades. And it might be the wrong one.

***

## The Orthodoxy: Something Must Be Wrong With the Rock

Here is the standard story. The continental crust, the outermost 30 to 50 kilometers of Earth's landmasses, is cold and rigid. When tectonic forces push it past its breaking point, it snaps. That snapping is an earthquake. Underneath the crust lies the mantle: hotter, under greater pressure, and composed of rock that, at those temperatures and pressures, should deform like extremely thick honey rather than fracture like glass.

The boundary between the two, the Mohorovicic discontinuity or "Moho," has long been treated as a kind of seismic floor. Earthquakes above it, yes. Earthquakes below it? Those require explanation.

And so the explanations came, all of them focused on finding ways to make mantle rock temporarily brittle:

**Dehydration embrittlement.** Water locked inside hydrous minerals like serpentine gets released during metamorphic reactions. The freed water raises pore fluid pressure, effectively weakening the rock enough to fracture. This mechanism is well-supported for subduction zones, where water-saturated oceanic crust plunges into the mantle. But continental mantle rock is generally much drier. Whether enough water exists at depth beneath continents to trigger this process remains an open debate.

**Thermal shear runaway.** Slow viscous creep in the mantle concentrates heat along a narrow band. The heat weakens the rock, which deforms faster, generating more heat, creating a feedback loop that ends in sudden failure. A study of seismic evidence from intermediate-depth earthquakes found stress drops roughly 100 times larger than apparent stress, consistent with temperature spikes of 600 to 1000°C in centimeter-thick layers during failure. In late 2025, a paper in *The Seismological Record* confirmed this as "a viable rupture mechanism for mantle earthquakes."

**Eclogitization-driven failure.** When lower-crustal rock enters the eclogite stability field during subduction, the phase transformation creates weak nanometric bands that self-organize into faults. But Schulte-Pelkum et al. showed in 2019 in *Geology* that Himalayan mantle earthquakes happen *below* where eclogitization occurs, ruling this out as a direct cause for the mantle events themselves.

Each of these mechanisms asks the same underlying question in different ways: *What special thing happens to the rock that makes it break?*

They are all, in a sense, material-property explanations. The assumption is that ductile mantle rock requires some exotic local transformation to become brittle before it can produce an earthquake. The mystery is then: which transformation? Under what conditions? And can we detect it?

But there is a second possibility that the field has largely overlooked.

## The Question Nobody Is Asking

What if mantle rock does not need to become brittle at all?

Ductile rock has a finite strength. It deforms gradually under most conditions, but it does not have infinite capacity to absorb stress. If enough stress accumulates in a localized area faster than the rock can dissipate it through creep, the rock fails. Not because it became brittle, but because it was simply overwhelmed.

The distinction matters. If the question is "how does ductile rock become brittle," you search for special mechanisms: water, phase transitions, thermal feedbacks. If the question is "how does enough stress concentrate in one spot to exceed even ductile rock's instantaneous yield strength," you search for something completely different: the geometry and timing of stress delivery.

This reframing changes everything about where you look for answers.

***

## The Mantle as Capacitor

To understand the alternative, consider how the crust and mantle actually interact after an earthquake.

When a large crustal earthquake occurs, say, a magnitude 7 along the Himalayan front, the sudden release of energy redistributes stress in all directions. Some of that stress propagates laterally through the crust. Some propagates downward into the lower crust and upper mantle. This is not speculative. It is well-documented through decades of post-seismic geodetic observations and finite-element modeling.

In 2005, Kenner and Simons published a landmark paper in *Geophysical Journal International* introducing the "Wallace Number," a dimensionless parameter that controls whether earthquakes on a fault tend to cluster in time or occur at regular intervals. The Wallace Number depends on the ratio of earthquake stress drop to the product of tectonic strain rate and effective lithospheric viscosity. Their key finding: the viscoelastic mantle acts as a stress reservoir. After a crustal earthquake, coseismic stress is transferred downward into the viscous lower crust and upper mantle. Over months to decades, that stress relaxes and flows back upward, reloading the crust and potentially triggering the next earthquake sooner than the tectonic loading rate alone would dictate.

Freed and Lin (2001) demonstrated this quantitatively for the Mojave Desert, showing that viscoelastic relaxation following the 1992 Landers earthquake transferred 1 to 2 bars of Coulomb stress to the future hypocenter of the 1999 Hector Mine earthquake over a period of seven years. The mantle did not just passively absorb the stress and forget about it. It stored it temporarily and redirected it.

Think of the mantle as an electrical capacitor. It receives a charge (coseismic stress), stores it temporarily, and then discharges it (post-seismic relaxation back to the crust). Each earthquake cycle charges and discharges the capacitor.

But here is the critical part that has been hiding in plain sight: **the discharge is never complete.**

## The Residual That Accumulates

Not all of the stress transferred into the mantle during a crustal earthquake returns to the crust. Some fraction remains. This is particularly true at **lateral viscosity gradients**, places where hotter, weaker mantle sits next to colder, stronger mantle.

Why? Because stress in a viscous medium flows toward regions of lower viscosity, where it can be dissipated more easily. But at the boundary between a high-viscosity and low-viscosity domain, there is a bottleneck. Stress arriving at the boundary from the high-viscosity side cannot flow sideways into the low-viscosity domain as easily as it relaxes vertically. It gets trapped.

Over one earthquake cycle, the trapped residual is negligible. Over hundreds or thousands of earthquake cycles spanning millions of years, it is not.

This is where a remarkable 2024 study becomes pivotal. Becker et al., published in *Communications Earth & Environment*, demonstrated that intraplate seismicity across western and central Europe is not randomly scattered. It clusters preferentially above **lateral gradients in upper mantle effective viscosity**. Not above the weakest patches. Not above the strongest patches. At the boundaries between the two.

Their interpretation focused on how mantle buoyancy forces create differential loading on the crust above. But the same data supports a second interpretation: those viscosity gradient zones are also the places where stress residuals from centuries of crustal earthquake cycles have accumulated within the mantle itself.

If that accumulation reaches the instantaneous yield strength of the local mantle rock, even ductile rock fails. No dehydration needed. No thermal runaway needed. No phase transition needed. Just geometry, time, and arithmetic.

## Why This Explains the Geographic Pattern

The Wang and Klemperer catalog shows 459 continental mantle earthquakes clustered in specific places: the Himalayas, the Bering Strait, the Alpine-Himalayan collision belt, East Africa. The Bering Strait cluster was especially unexpected, because nobody had previously identified mantle earthquakes there.

Every existing explanation struggles with this geographic pattern. If dehydration embrittlement were the cause, you would expect CMEs wherever hydrous minerals exist at depth. If thermal runaway were the cause, you would expect CMEs wherever the mantle is hot enough for vigorous creep. Neither prediction matches the observed clustering.

But the stress-capacitor model predicts exactly what we see.

The Himalayas sit atop the most seismically active continental collision zone on Earth. Thousands of crustal earthquakes have occurred there over millions of years. The Indian mantle lithosphere beneath Tibet has been torn into at least four pieces, as demonstrated by Liang et al. (2018) in *PNAS*. Each fragment boundary is a lateral viscosity contrast. Millennia of crustal earthquake stress have been pumped downward into this geometrically fractured mantle, accumulating at every fragment edge.

The Bering Strait sits at a transition between the thick, cold, strong lithosphere of the North American and Eurasian cratons and the thinner, warmer lithosphere beneath the Bering Sea. That transition is, by definition, a lateral viscosity gradient.

East Africa, where continental rifting is actively thinning the lithosphere, is riddled with lateral viscosity contrasts between the hot rift axis and the cold, intact lithosphere on either side.

In every case, the pattern is the same: **CMEs cluster not where the mantle has special properties, but where stress has had both a long time and a clear geometric pathway to accumulate.**

## What the Conventional Framework Cannot Explain

The 2017 Wyoming Craton earthquake makes the case sharply. A magnitude 4.8 event originated at roughly 75 km depth, at estimated temperatures above 750°C, well past the canonical brittle-ductile transition. The earthquake's characteristics were deeply unusual: high stress drop, low rupture velocity, and low radiation efficiency. The authors of the study, published in *Nature Geoscience*, described this as evidence of a "dissipative" failure mechanism that does not fit neatly into any existing category.

Neither dehydration embrittlement nor eclogitization can easily explain an earthquake in dry cratonic mantle far from any subduction zone. Thermal shear runaway is plausible in principle but requires active deformation to initiate the feedback loop. What drives such deformation in a stable craton?

The stress-capacitor hypothesis offers an answer. Cratonic lithosphere is old, cold, and strong. It has high effective viscosity. Lateral viscosity gradients within cratons are subtle but real, reflecting ancient suture zones, compositional boundaries from billions of years of accretion, and thermal anomalies from long-dead mantle plumes. Stress residuals from even rare and small crustal earthquakes in these settings accumulate very slowly but very persistently, because the high viscosity means almost nothing dissipates. Given enough time, hundreds of millions of years in some cratons, even small per-cycle residuals can sum to significant stress concentrations at internal boundary zones.

The craton is the slowest capacitor on Earth, but it has been charging the longest.

## A Testable Prediction

The value of a hypothesis is not in its elegance but in what it predicts that existing frameworks do not.

If the mantle-capacitor model is correct, the following should be true:

**The majority of the 459 cataloged continental mantle earthquakes should fall within 50 km laterally of a significant upper mantle viscosity contrast, defined as a factor of 3 or greater change in effective viscosity over 100 km horizontal distance.**

This is testable right now, using the existing Wang-Klemperer catalog and published shear-wave tomography models that have already been converted to effective viscosity maps (as Becker et al. did for Europe in 2024). Extending that analysis globally, using models like SL2013sv or other full-waveform inversion products, would allow a direct spatial correlation test.

Here are the specific falsification criteria:

1. **If fewer than 60% of cataloged CMEs fall within 50 km of a significant viscosity gradient**, the hypothesis is weakened. The gradient-trapping mechanism alone cannot explain the majority of events.

2. **If CMEs are uniformly distributed within broad low-viscosity or high-viscosity domains rather than concentrated at their boundaries**, the stress-trapping mechanism collapses entirely. Stress would be dissipating evenly rather than accumulating at edges.

3. **If mantle earthquakes show no temporal correlation with prior crustal seismicity in the same region over decadal timescales**, the stress-recycling loop from crust to mantle is not the primary driver. This could be tested by looking at whether CME occurrence rates in a region increase in the decades following large crustal earthquake sequences.

No existing mechanism makes predictions this specific about the geographic distribution of continental mantle earthquakes relative to mantle rheological structure. Dehydration embrittlement predicts correlation with hydration state. Thermal runaway predicts correlation with temperature. Eclogitization predicts correlation with metamorphic facies boundaries. The capacitor model predicts correlation with **viscosity gradient geometry**, which is a structurally distinct observable.

## What Changes If This Is Right

If the mantle-capacitor model holds up, several implications follow:

**The search for exotic embrittlement mechanisms becomes less urgent for the bulk of CMEs.** Individual events may still involve thermal runaway or dehydration as the final failure mechanism at the moment of rupture. But the cause of the earthquake, the reason stress was high enough for any failure mechanism to activate, is not the material transformation. It is the cumulative geometric stress trap.

This is analogous to how a dam failure works. The final breach may occur through erosion, seepage, structural fatigue, or overtopping. Those are the mechanisms. But the cause is that too much water was stored behind the dam. Studying only the breach mechanism without understanding the water budget gives you an incomplete picture.

**Mantle earthquake hazard assessment becomes possible, in principle.** If CMEs are driven by stress accumulation at viscosity gradients, then regions with both active crustal seismicity and sharp lateral viscosity contrasts in the upper mantle are the highest-risk areas. This is an assessable criterion, unlike "places where dehydration reactions might occur at exactly the right depth."

**The "jelly sandwich" vs. "creme brulee" debate gets reframed.** The question of whether the continental mantle is "strong" or "weak" may be less important than the question of whether the continental mantle has sharp internal viscosity boundaries. A uniformly strong mantle (pure jelly sandwich) with no lateral viscosity gradients would have fewer stress traps and thus fewer CMEs than a heterogeneous mantle with complex internal structure, regardless of its average strength.

**The interconnected earthquake cycle that Wang proposed becomes mechanically specified.** Wang told reporters that "continental mantle earthquakes might be part of an inherently interconnected earthquake cycle, both from the crust and also the upper mantle." The capacitor model gives that idea a concrete physical mechanism: crustal earthquakes charge the mantle; mantle viscosity gradients trap the residual; when the residual exceeds instantaneous yield strength, the mantle produces its own earthquakes, which in turn redistribute stress back into the crust and into adjacent mantle regions.

***

## The Uncomfortable Implication

There is a reason nobody has proposed this before, despite the pieces being available in the literature for years.

The stress-capacitor model implies that continental mantle earthquakes are not anomalies to be explained away by special mechanisms. They are the expected, inevitable consequence of any system where stress is repeatedly transferred from a brittle layer into a viscous one with internal geometric heterogeneity, given enough time.

They are not "impossible earthquakes" or "mysterious deep quakes." They are the mantle's receipts for every crustal earthquake that ever happened above them.

That framing is uncomfortable because it dissolves the mystique. Geophysicists have spent two decades constructing elaborate laboratory experiments and numerical models to understand how mantle rock can be made brittle. The capacitor model says: maybe it does not need to be. Maybe the answer was always about the geometry of the plumbing, not the chemistry of the pipes.

The 459 earthquakes in the Wang-Klemperer catalog are a starting point. They are also, potentially, the first direct evidence that the mantle keeps a running tab of crustal seismicity, payable at viscosity gradient boundaries, on geological timescales.

The question was never "how does the rock break?"

The question is: "how long has this particular stretch of mantle been getting charged?"
