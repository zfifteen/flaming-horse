The Antarctic ozone hole's remarkable predictability (consistent timing and severity year-to-year) emerges not from atmospheric simplicity, but from operating in a chemically saturated regime where chlorine loading and cold temperatures exceed critical thresholds by wide margins, making depletion inevitable despite moderate meteorological variations.

As chlorine concentrations decline during the 2030-2050 recovery period, the Antarctic system will transition from this saturated regime toward threshold-limited behavior, making ozone hole characteristics increasingly sensitive to year-to-year weather variations.

This predicts a counterintuitive pattern: interannual variability in ozone hole severity will increase during recovery, even as average conditions improve, because the system loses its current "over-determined" character where multiple redundant chemical pathways ensure consistent destruction.

Current Antarctic behavior resembles the Arctic pattern 40 years in the future, while future Antarctic behavior will become more Arctic-like in its unpredictability.

Recovery monitoring should expect and communicate that erratic, highly variable ozone hole behavior from year to year represents progress, not setback, as the system transitions away from the extreme conditions that currently guarantee consistent severe depletion.

Quantitatively, year-to-year variability should increase by at least 25% during the 2030-2050 period compared to 2000-2025, measured as the coefficient of variation in minimum springtime column ozone.

This matters because public and scientific expectations of "smooth recovery" could mistake increased variability for failure of the Montreal Protocol, when it actually signals the system moving away from the saturated extreme that currently produces predictable destruction.
